What is an estimate?

Rough calculation or judgement regarding the value, number, quantity or extent of…

Before we start into the meat of the topic, I wanted to make sure that we can all agree on what an estimate is? The dictionary calls it a: Rough calculation or judgement regarding the value, number, quantity or extent of something Now, what that something actually is depends on your situation but in all instances estimates are approximations, guesses or evaluations based on something else. Seems quite unnerving to think we base the future of our businesses on something so brittle as a guess.

Estimation issues

  1. Harmful and personal
  2. Binary and Compound
  3. Incomplete...

OK, I admit it. I said I had three points, but this one has two in it. Sorry :)

  1. Let's start with an easy one. Estimates are harmful because they build a bridge into the non-agile planning world and thus prevent the transition to an agile product development and personal because more often than not, the person completing the work doesn't create the estimate.

  2. I'm sure you have all been in the situation where you've estimated the features which have been agreed to go into the next sprint. Excellent, job done! Hopefully. I see a problem here.

    These problems are two-fold: Each item you estimate with a single figure value has a 50% chance of success. Like the toss of a fair coin. It then has a binary state, either success or failure. Anything up to and including your estimated time is success any even one minute beyond it equals failure. This 'feature' becomes coumpounded when you add individual items together to form something larger... a Sprint, for example. Basing the sprint on multiple estimates changes the equation from how likely is it to throw one head to; How likely is it to throw heads 10 times in a row? 1000 to one... that's a 0.09% chance of success. Yay estimation!

Don't estimate, forecast

Hopefully you can see now, that communicating predictions based on single figure estimates sets you up to fail.

Give yourself, and your teams, a fighting chance.

What is a forecast?

To calculate or predict (some future event or condition) usually as a result of study and analysis of available pertinent data

At the start of the session, we looked at the definition of an estimate. Let's compare it to a forecast

To calculate or predict (some future event or condition) usually as a result of study and analysis of available pertinent data
Synonyms: budget, outlook, prognosis, projection. They feel more positive and measured.

Heijunka: Level the flow

One thing which people ask about, when I describe this method to them, is the inconsistency in effort required per card/issue/etc which would mean forecasting won't work. Firstly, small variations in size won't matter. The forecast range will tak them into account. If you're talking about larger differences, then, we need to follow the basic rules for writing user stories which pretty much all scrum masters have been promoting for years. Cards should be small enough to complete within an iteration... and if you want to complete more than one thing per iteration or have more than one person working at a times they need to be smaller than that. Ideally, to limit the impact of context switching, stories should be completed within one working day. Hopefully, this is nothing new to anyone in the room. So, just as in Lean flow, as you reduce variation you will get predicability and forecasting becomes more useful. This will happen natually during a project, for example, I've experienced a team who, once we'd stopped estimating, were consistently completing between 10 and 12 cards per week. This gave us predictability and as long as the team were involved in the creation of the cards, the status quo was kept.
